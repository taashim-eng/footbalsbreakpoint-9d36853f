The Break Point · Forensic Statistics Dossier

Statistical review v2 · adversarially re-examined · 2002–2026

The obvious story is wrong. The real one is about heat and inequality, not a fix.

The dashboard was built on one claim: lower-GDP nations collapse during the 65′–80′ hydration break. Pooled across every match, that claim is a confounded, underpowered non-finding. Then a five-persona adversarial review pulled a thread loose. In the hottest matches, poorer teams concede far more even after you adjust for how good they are. That reads as a resource and physiology story, not manipulation, and it hides in a blind spot our goal-based data can't see into.

Bottom line No sign of a break-specific fix. A heat × economics signal does survive, as an exploratory lead. Read it as unequal preparation for the heat, not a conspiracy. The integrity risk that is actually documented lives somewhere else.

01 · The claim

Poorer nations seem to leak goals in the break

We kept only the 308 genuine cross-group matches: exactly one higher-GDP ("Group A") team against one lower-GDP ("Group B") team. Then we counted, from real minute-by-minute event data, who conceded during the 65′–80′ window. The raw contrast is the kind of number that launches an investigation.

+29% Lower-GDP teams conceded in the break window in 21.4% of matches, against 16.6% for higher-GDP teams. That is a rate ratio of 1.29. Suggestive. But "suggestive" is where the work starts, not where it ends.

A responsible analyst treats that first number as a hypothesis, then asks it to survive progressively harder tests. Each rung below removes one innocent explanation for the gap.

Pooled across every match, the signal erodes. It's weakly present when nothing is controlled, then not distinguishable from zero once you compare like with like. The first version of this report stopped here and called it "debunked." The next two sections show why that was too quick.

03 · The pooled picture in one chart

The GDP estimates hug the line. Then heat splits the room

ELI5 · confidence interval

A confidence interval is the honest version of a single number: the range the true value plausibly sits in, given how much data we have. If it includes 1.0 (for a ratio), we can't rule out "nothing is happening." Think of guessing a height as "5′2″ to 6′4″". Honest, but too wide to call. The pooled GDP bars are exactly that. The amber hot-match bar is the one that finally stands apart from the fence.

04 · The honest limit of a null

"Not significant" is not the same as "nothing there"

Before celebrating a null, ask what it could even have detected. With 308 matches and a ~17% base rate, this study can only reliably catch a rate ratio above 1.55. The observed effect is 1.29, below that floor. A formal equivalence test (TOST) confirms the asymmetry. We can statistically rule out a doubling of the concession rate (IRR 2.0, p = 0.011), but we cannot rule out a 50% increase (IRR 1.5, p = 0.24).

So the pooled result is precise: no evidence of a break-window GDP effect, not proof there is none. The original report's "explained away" over-reached. This is an underpowered null with room for a moderate real effect.

ELI5 · statistical power & equivalence

Absence of evidence isn't evidence of absence. Search a dark room with a dim torch and find nothing, and you haven't proven the room is empty. Your torch was just too weak to catch a small object. "Power" is how bright your torch is, and a small sample is a dim one. An equivalence test is the honest flashlight check: it can only declare "nothing meaningful here" if the beam was strong enough to have seen it. Ours could spot an elephant (a doubling) but not a large dog (a 50% rise).

05 · The heat turn

Where the review changed the answer

The sports scientist on the panel made the sharpest point: cooling breaks are heat-triggered. If they matter, the effect should live in hot matches, not the average one. Testing that flipped the story:

  • The GDP effect grows with temperature: a significant heat × GDP interaction (IRR 1.13 per °C, p = 0.024).
  • In the hottest third of matches, a lower-GDP team concedes in the break window at 2.1× the higher-GDP rate. Crucially, this survives adjustment for FIFA ranking (IRR 2.21, 95% CI [1.18, 4.15], p = 0.014). It is not merely team strength this time.
  • But within those hot matches the difference-in-differences (+0.03, CI [−0.17, +0.21]) spans zero, so it is not shown to be break-specific. It reads as general heat vulnerability, not a cooling-break collapse.

Read plainly: in the heat, poorer-nation sides concede more even after accounting for how good they are. We just can't pin it to the break itself, and our "hot" cut (WBGT ≈ 27°C) is milder than the ~32°C that actually triggers a cooling break. Treat it as a real, pre-registerable lead, not a confirmed finding. It is one uncorrected subgroup, reported as exploratory.

ELI5 · interaction & the forking-paths trap

An interaction is "it depends." Sunscreen's benefit depends on sun; here, GDP's effect depends on heat. That's a real, testable idea. The danger is that if you slice data enough ways, one slice will look exciting by luck. Statisticians call it the "garden of forking paths." That's why we label this exploratory and say it needs a fresh, pre-committed test before anyone believes it. Confirmatory conclusions and interesting leads are different currencies, and we don't spend one as the other. It also invites an obvious objection, which the next section takes head on.

06 · The acclimatisation objection

"But poorer nations are hot-climate nations, shouldn't heat help them?"

A fair challenge. Distance runners from the Kenyan highlands carry an altitude edge everywhere they race. If poorer footballing nations come from hotter homes, heat should be their advantage, not their undoing, which would turn §05 on its head. So we tested it directly, adding each nation's home climate (absolute latitude of its capital, where lower means more tropical) to the models.

QuestionTestResultVerdict

Does the heat lead survive home climate?Hot-match GDP effect, ± climate2.21 → 2.19, p 0.014 → 0.056Now borderline

The premise is right: poorer sides here are from significantly hotter climates. The predicted payoff never materialises, though. Home climate has no protective effect in the heat, and hot-climate teams do not concede less. The acclimatisation edge that helps Kenyan runners simply doesn't show up for these footballers.

Why does the analogy break? Elite footballers aren't continuously acclimatised to their national climate. They play club seasons across the globe (a Senegalese star spends winters in Europe), so "home latitude" is a weak stand-in for a squad's real heat readiness, unlike a runner who trains at altitude every day. World-Cup venues are neutral and rarely reach the ~32°C that makes acclimatisation decisive, and our "hot" cut is only ~27°C. Any edge is plausibly swamped by the very resource gaps §05 points to. And because GDP and home climate are collinear (r = −0.59), they compete for the same variance: the hot-match GDP effect barely moves when climate is added (2.21 → 2.19) yet slips just past significance (p = 0.056). Home climate doesn't explain it away. It does mean we can't cleanly credit "poverty" versus "climate." So the honest label for the §05 lead tightens from "exploratory" to exploratory and under-identified.

ELI5 · acclimatisation & collinearity

Acclimatisation is your body adapting to the conditions it lives in, like mountain air or relentless heat. The intuition that hot-country teams should shrug off hot games is reasonable. It just isn't visible here, because footballers live and play all over the world, not at home.

Collinearity is two clues that always travel together, so you can't tell which one is talking. "Poorer" and "from a hotter country" are nearly the same teams (they move together with correlation −0.59). Ask a model to split their credit for the heat effect and it throws up its hands. Not because there's nothing there, but because the two are too tangled to separate with this much data.

07 · What "GDP" is really measuring

A crude label with a humane explanation

The sociologist's warning matters. Splitting nations at the median GDP is a blunt instrument. It conflates economic development with footballing strength: Brazil and Argentina are football-rich without being GDP-rich, and strong African sides land in "Group B." That is why FIFA ranking, a football-specific measure, behaves so much more cleanly in every model, and why the pooled "GDP effect" is mostly ranking wearing a disguise.

But the hot-match signal that survives ranking asks for a different reading. The plausible, benign mechanism is resource inequality. Heat acclimatisation camps, sports-science and recovery budgets, squad depth and substitution quality all track national wealth, and all blunt late-game defensive fatigue in extreme heat. That is a story about preparation gaps, not fixed matches. It is exactly the kind of structural inequality a responsible analysis should name rather than dress up as an "anomaly."

ELI5 · construct validity

A ruler should measure the thing you care about. If you want "how good at football," national GDP is a bent ruler, because rich doesn't mean good at this sport. FIFA ranking is the straighter one. When a bent-ruler result (GDP) survives only after you've also held the straight ruler (ranking) fixed, whatever's left is telling you about something GDP captures that ranking doesn't. Most likely, money spent preparing bodies for heat.

08 · Keeping ourselves honest

Many tests, one strict bar

Run enough tests and one will look "significant" by luck. Take the pre-specified confirmatory family: the unpaired, paired and adjusted GDP tests, plus the three per-era subgroup tests the review insisted we include. A Benjamini-Hochberg correction leaves the smallest q-value at 0.245. Nothing in the confirmatory set survives. The lone era subgroup that looked significant on its own (Era C, p = 0.046) does not clear the corrected bar. The heat lead of §05 is deliberately kept outside this family, as exploratory.

ELI5 · multiple comparisons

Flip twenty coins and one will probably show a streak. That's luck, because you gave it twenty tries. Testing many hypotheses is the same. The correction is a stricter bar that scales with how many peeks you took, so a fluke among many doesn't get crowned a discovery.

10 · The overlay you asked for

Where the real integrity risk actually lives

We were asked to weigh proven corruption, gambling and broadcast money against the on-pitch numbers. The honest map separates motive from evidence, and shows our analysis is pointed at the wrong surface to catch what has actually been caught.

$

The motive is enormous, and real

FIFA booked $7.57 bn for the 2019–22 cycle (45% from broadcast rights) and projects $11–13 bn for 2026. Global World-Cup wagering runs from tens of billions (regulated) to a UNODC-estimated $1.7 tn illegal market. Big-market and host teams advancing lifts all of it.

Motive ≠ evidence
§

Proven FIFA corruption is administrative

The 2015 US DOJ case (~$150 m in bribes) was about media & marketing rights, bids and elections, not rigging match results. Blatter and Platini were later acquitted of criminal fraud. The money is stolen in boardrooms, not on the pitch.

Off-pitch
⚑

Proven match-fixing sits in the lower tiers

Documented cases target qualifiers, friendlies and officials: Nepal's 2011 qualifiers, life bans for referees (Chaibou) and coaches (Siasia). Not the World-Cup-finals matches in our 2002–2022 sample.

Out of sample
◉

Modern risk is invisible to us

2026's integrity flags are spot-fixing (cards, corners, throw-ins, such as the Wahi referral) and refereeing disputes (Egypt's complaint over Argentina's Round of 16). A final-score / goals analysis like ours cannot see either. Allegations are unproven and unadjudicable from scorelines.

Blind spot

So the strongest incentives and the actually-documented failures both concentrate in places our numbers don't reach: administrative money, prop markets, and officiating. That is a reason for humility about scope, not an inference of guilt. Sources are catalogued in backend/analysis/EXTERNAL_FACTS.md.

Verdict

An anomaly is a deviation from expectation, not evidence of wrongdoing. Pooled, the break-window GDP pattern is confounded by team strength and too underpowered to call either way. Conditioned on heat, a disadvantage for poorer nations appears, but it is entangled with those nations' hot home climates and so under-identified. We can say hot-climate teams get no protective edge (the acclimatisation objection fails), yet we cannot cleanly credit poverty versus climate for what remains. The most defensible reading is unequal readiness for heat, not a fix, and not clearly tied to the break itself. Meanwhile the corruption that is documented lives in boardrooms, prop bets and officiating, surfaces a scoreline model cannot inspect. The correct report is the careful one: name the plausible inequality, keep the promising lead labelled as a lead, and refuse to let a striking chart imply a story the data won't support.

11 · The live tournament

2026 has no event data, so we measure surprise instead

The 2026 World Cup is in progress, and we hold only observed final scores for the 96 completed matches. No minute-level events, no market data. Rather than fabricate a trajectory, we attach one honest, self-contained metric to every game: a shock index. A Poisson model (with a Dixon-Coles low-score correction) learns each team's scoring and conceding rate so far, shrunk toward the tournament average of 2.92 goals/match, then predicts the fixture and measures how surprised it is by the real result. "Model xG" here is that rate-based expectation, not shot-based expected goals.

ELI5 · shock index (surprisal)

Imagine betting on the exact score with a well-informed friend. If the game ends as expected, you shrug. If it ends 7–1, you gasp. The shock index is a number for that gasp: the "surprise" (negative log-probability) the model assigns to the real score. Blowouts and out-of-nowhere results score high, routine 1–0s score low.

Shock ≠ upset. Paraguay knocking out Germany was the headline, but on the pitch it finished 1–1, an unremarkable scoreline. The drama was the penalty shootout, which a goals model can't see. For "David beat Goliath," read the upset table.

13 · Methods & limitations

What this analysis is, and what it isn't

  • Real data only. Historical figures come from observed goal events (jfjelstul World Cup database, 2002–2022, plus continental cups 2003–2019). 2026 is a one-time scrape of completed matches. No values are simulated.
  • Break occurrence is unobserved. The 65′–80′ window is a fixed proxy; real cooling breaks are heat-triggered (~32°C WBGT) and taken ~30′ and ~75′. We cannot verify which historical matches actually had a break, a core limitation behind the "not break-specific" caveat.
  • Home climate is a coarse proxy. Acclimatisation is approximated by absolute capital latitude, which ignores altitude, humidity and coastal effects, and cannot capture where a squad's players actually live and train year-round. It is a first-order test of the objection, not the last word.
  • Blind to spot-fixing & officiating. Our unit is goals/scorelines; the documented modern risks (props, referees) leave no scoreline signature. "No anomaly" speaks only to result manipulation via goals.
  • Betting signals excluded. The project's earlier odds/volume series were synthetic (no licensed feed) and are not used; any remaining modeled odds in the dashboard are labelled as such.
  • Observational, not causal. Even the ranking and heat effects are associations with residual-confounding risk. External corruption/revenue/gambling figures are context only, never joined to matches.
  • Deterministic & reproducible. Fixed seed (42); cluster-robust SEs by match; 5,000-sample match-level bootstrap; Dixon-Coles ρ = −0.10 fixed (not fitted at this n).

14 · Plain-English glossary

Every concept and metric, in one line

p-value "could this be luck?"
The probability of seeing a gap this big or bigger if nothing were really going on. Small (<0.05) means "hard to explain by chance."
Statistical power "how bright is the torch?"
The chance your study could detect a real effect of a given size. Low power (small samples) means a "null" might just be a dim torch missing a real thing. Ours could detect a doubling, but not a 50% rise.
Equivalence test (TOST) "is it truly negligible?"
Flips the usual question: instead of "is there an effect?", it asks "can we rule out an effect bigger than X?". Only then may you claim "nothing meaningful here."
Effect size / Cramér's V "how big, not just is-it-there"
Significance asks whether; effect size asks how much. Cramér's V runs 0–1. Ours is 0.06, trivially small.
Rate ratio / IRR "how many times more often"
1.0 = identical. Ranking IRR 1.30 = a standard-deviation-weaker team concedes ~30% more. The hot-match GDP IRR of 2.21 clears 1.0; the pooled 1.32 does not.
Confidence interval "the honest range"
The band of plausible true values. Crossing the no-effect line (1.0 for ratios, 0 for differences) = consistent with nothing.
Interaction "it depends"
When one factor's effect changes with another. Here GDP's effect on concessions grows with heat, a significant interaction and the crux of the surviving lead.
Difference-in-differences "is the break special?"
Each team's change from its own pre-break baseline, then compared. Isolates a break-specific effect from simply being weaker. Ours spans zero.
Confounding & construct validity "the hidden cause; the bent ruler"
Confounding: a third variable (team strength) fakes a link. Construct validity: whether GDP even measures "football strength." It doesn't, cleanly; ranking does.
FDR correction "don't reward luck"
A stricter bar for running many tests (Benjamini-Hochberg), so a chance hit among many isn't crowned a discovery.
Dixon-Coles / Poisson shock index "the size of the gasp"
Poisson models goal counts; Dixon-Coles corrects its known mispricing of low, correlated scores (0–0, 1–1). Surprisal = −log P(actual score): rare results score high. A surprising scoreline, not necessarily an upset.
Acclimatisation & collinearity "adapted to conditions; clues that travel together"
Acclimatisation: bodies adapting to their usual environment (heat, altitude). Collinearity: two variables so correlated (here poverty & hot home, r = −0.59) that a model can't tell which is driving an effect. That's why the heat lead is "under-identified."
Spot-fixing "rigging the small stuff"
Manipulating in-game events (a card, a corner, a throw-in) for prop bets, without changing who wins. Invisible to a final-score analysis, and where current World-Cup integrity flags actually sit.
